The 4 P’s of Marketing: A Breakdown

The 4 P’s of marketing can help you with every aspect of your marketing strategy – whether you’re having your website designed, or working on a social media campaign. In this article, we’ll discuss the 4 P’s of marketing and how to use this marketing mix to create a winning marketing strategy for your business in 2023.

What are the 4 P’s of marketing?

In order to ensure the successful marketing of a product or service, it is necessary to carefully review and appropriately apply the four pillars – product, price, place, and promotion.

The 4 P’s of marketing are often referred to as the marketing mix. They cover a range of factors that are taken into account when marketing a product, including what consumers want, how the product or service meets their needs or does not meet their needs, how the product or service is perceived in the world, how it stands out from the competition, and how the company that produces the product or service is interacting with its customers.

The concept of the marketing mix was popularised by Neil Borden, a professor of advertising at the Harvard Graduate School of Business Administration, in the 1960s. Since then, more P’s have been identified and are commonly used – people, process, and physical evidence.

4 p's of marketing - product, price, place and promotion

A breakdown of the 4 P’s of marketing

Below we will cover the following marketing mix items – product, price, place, and promotion.

1. Product

This one is easy and simple – products refer to the goods and services that businesses offer to consumers, with the intention of satisfying the consumer’s wants or needs. Every marketing campaign starts with understanding the product (or service) you are selling. Marketers need to clearly define the product and its qualities before introducing it to the customer.

In addition to understanding the product and having knowledge of your target customers and your competitors, you should also be familiar with your product’s life cycle—meaning its introduction into the market, its growth, maturity, and decline. From a marketing standpoint, this lets you adapt your messaging, pricing, and target audience according to each life cycle stage.

To start truly understanding your product, ask yourself these questions:

  • What is my product?
  • How and where do customers use my product?
  • Does my product satisfy any existing needs? If it doesn’t, how will I create a new need in the market?
  • What exact features does my product have that will directly address those needs? Are there any features I may have missed that my customers would want or need?
  • What is my product called?
  • How will I brand my product?
  • How does my product differ from the competition? What makes my product stand out?

The answers to these questions can help you make your marketing plan a huge success.

2. Price

The price is simply what your consumers pay for your product/service. That sounds very simple, and it truly is, but what makes it complicated is selecting a price that takes into consideration your business expenses, your profit margins, how much your consumers value your product, and your competitor’s pricing.

The reason price is an important part of your marketing mix, is that the price of your product affects the messaging you will use to promote your product, the kinds of consumers you will market to, and how accessible your product needs to be.

Ask yourself these questions to help you narrow down your pricing:

  • How does my price compare with that of my competitors?
  • Is my audience price-sensitive? Will decreasing the price help me capture more customers, or will it decrease my profit margins? How about vis-vera?
  • Can I use different pricing tiers (for instance, a basic versus a premium package) for different customer segments?
  • What is the perceived value of my good or service to my customers?

Think about how you will align your marketing strategy with your customer’s perceived value of your product.

3. Place

“Place”, the third P of the marketing mix, refers to the location of your marketing and distribution efforts. It can be a physical location – a brick-and-mortar- that targets local communities, for example. an outlet in your local mall or business center. Or it can be an online location, such as the various online platforms that you can use to promote and sell your products or services – as such your own custom website or social media channels such as TikTok.

Deciding where your products will be available is a very important decision. Think about luxury brands. Would their products be located in high-end neighborhoods where the community has more disposable income available to spend on luxury products or would they open a store in a low-income neighborhood? The answer is clear and is very simple – make your products available where you are more likely to sell them.

The same applies online. You need to market your products where your target consumers hang out. This means looking at the online platforms that exist and researching their users. Then you can narrow down which platforms you will be using to market your products based on where your target audience spends their time and money.

Consider the questions below:

  • Where is my target audience located (both in person or online)?
  • Where do people look for my product? If they look in a store, what kind (e.g., a boutique versus a large chain)? If they look online, what platforms are they using?
  • What kinds of distribution channels and outlets can I use to sell my product?
  • Where are my competitors selling? What physical locations and online channels are they using?

Answering these questions may help you figure out where the best place is to start marketing your business.

4. Promotion

Promotion is the fourth P of the marketing mix. This is where everything we mentioned above comes together. Promotion refers to the activities that a company undertakes to communicate and promote its products or services to its target audience. Promotion includes activities such as advertising, public relations, and the overall media strategy for introducing a product.

A few questions you can ask yourself are:

  • Where can I get my message across to my target market? Should I use online channels like social media? Maybe PR campaigns or TV ads? Perhaps radio or podcasts would be a better fit? What about the good old print advertisements or email marketing?
  • What sort of language, media, and personas does my audience connect to most in these online and offline locations?
  • When is the best time to promote my product or service? Is my product seasonal? Are there events or other external factors that might affect my messaging?
  • How, when, and where do my competitors promote their products?

That’s it! Those are the 4 P’s of marketing that make up the marketing mix. Use the information in this blog to help you put together your marketing strategy.
